Design of Everyday Things, Don Norman

overview

This book is popular among the software developer crowd (think the UX version of Lean Startup).

The premise of this book is that many things are designed poorly for the end-user.

This bit, 'for the end-user', is important. Why?

Many of the things designed poorly for the end-user are also

  • finely wrought
  • expensive
  • fashionable

These good qualities mask an important bad quality: they suck to use.

Norman's antidote: 'human-centered design' (i.e. do not make your product suck to use).

things I don't like about the book

'Design of Everyday Things' is not well written and unenjoyable to read. It falls into the category of a Blinkist book, that is, a book with 15 minutes of ideas spread across 200 pages of bad writing.

Norman also goes about inventing unhelpful vocabulary:

"I call the signalling component of affordances signifiers" [13]

"I call the combined information available to us the system image" [31]

good ideas gone ubiquitous

Norman offers some rules of thumb for designers:

  • controls should be clearly mapped (by proximity) to what they control [136]
  • give feedback to the user [156]
  • avoid adding too many features (aka 'featuritis') [261]

These rules seem pretty well understood, even by non-UX people. Should you even bother reading the book?

If 'Design of Everyday Things' is less useful to read in 2018 because all its ideas are already gospel.

Then again, given how often we make UX mistakes, and how little devlopers seem to care about providing a good experience to other developers, Norman still merits a look (esp. if you're the person directly in charge of making sure the product doesn't suck to use).

The experience of reading this book is kinda like reading Dale Carnegie's 'How to Win Friends and Influence People'; their messages can wear thin but repeating this stuff is helpful.
